Gender-Based Issues in Late ADHD Diagnosis Research

Health Sciences Qualitative Research GWU Phenomenological Study

Research Abstract

This qualitative study explored the psychological and social impacts of late diagnosis of attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) in adult women aged 18 and older. Utilizing in-depth interviews with 30 participants diagnosed after the age of 18, this research explored personal narratives to understand the barriers to early diagnosis and the subsequent effects on personal and professional life. Findings suggested that delayed diagnosis led to prolonged periods of mismanagement and significant emotional distress. Implications for clinical practice and policy reforms were discussed to enhance timely identification and support for women with ADHD.

Research Methodology

  • Study Design: Phenomenological qualitative approach
  • Sample Size: 30 adult women diagnosed with ADHD after age 18
  • Data Collection: Semi-structured in-depth interviews (60-90 minutes)
  • Analysis Method: Thematic analysis using NVivo software
  • Ethical Approval: George Washington University IRB

Key Findings

  • Gender bias in diagnostic criteria leading to underdiagnosis in females
  • Masking behaviors developed by women to cope with undiagnosed symptoms
  • Significant impact on academic and career trajectories
  • Mental health comorbidities resulting from years of misdiagnosis
  • Relief and validation experienced upon receiving diagnosis

Clinical Implications

  • Need for gender-sensitive diagnostic tools and criteria
  • Importance of screening adult women presenting with anxiety/depression
  • Training healthcare providers on female ADHD presentation
  • Development of support systems for newly diagnosed adults

Publication & Presentation

This research was completed as part of my Doctorate in Health Sciences at George Washington University (2024). Findings have been presented at healthcare conferences and are being prepared for publication in peer-reviewed journals focusing on women's health and neurodevelopmental disorders.
